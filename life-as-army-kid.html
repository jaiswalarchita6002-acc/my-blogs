Life as an Army Kid

BLOGS by ARCHITA JAISWAL – 12 April 2025

Being an Indian Army kid feels like living in a world that’s part disciplined, part adventurous. Life moves every two to three years with a new posting, so just when you get used to one place, it’s time to pack up and start over. At first, it feels tough—leaving behind friends, schools, and hangout spots—but soon you realize it’s also a gift. You get to see India in a way most people never do, hopping from one corner of the country to another. Each posting brings new adventures, new friends, and a whole new lifestyle.

Cantonments are honestly their own little universes. They’re massive—so huge that even after three years of living inside one, there are still lanes, grounds, or random corners you haven’t discovered. It’s peaceful, green, and safe, like a bubble away from the chaos of city life. And the best part? People here aren’t constantly rushing like they do in cities. There’s no mad traffic, no honking horns, no endless hurry. Instead, there’s a sense of calm—you see families walking in the evenings, kids playing outside till late, uncles chatting by the parks, and everyone seems a little happier, a little less stressed. It almost feels like time slows down here.

And of course, the privileges are the cherry on top. Groceries from the CSD canteen at super cheap rates, medical facilities right inside the gate, snacks and ice creams at prices that feel stuck in the 90s, and yes—free cinema tickets for weekend shows. Watching movies in the station theatre, with families laughing and clapping together, is a memory almost every army kid carries.

Then there’s Army Public School life, something almost every army kid relates to. Morning assemblies feel almost picture-perfect, with everyone in neat uniforms, lines straight as rulers, and the echo of patriotic songs filling the ground. Songs like “Saare Jahan Se Achha” or “Hum Honge Kamyab” sung in unison hit differently when you’re standing among hundreds of other kids, all belonging to the same lifestyle, the same discipline. And honestly, if you ever see someone getting annoyed because a school assembly isn’t proper, or a flag isn’t folded correctly—just know, that person is definitely an ex-APS student. The discipline stays with you forever.

Of course, it’s not all easy. With one parent often away on field duty, birthdays or school events sometimes feel incomplete. But that also makes you stronger, more independent, and more grateful for the moments when the whole family is together.

In the end, the army kid lifestyle is one-of-a-kind. The friendships you build, the peace you breathe, the little perks you enjoy, and the big cantonment life—it all shapes you in ways nothing else can. And honestly, even with its challenges, it’s a childhood you’d never trade.
